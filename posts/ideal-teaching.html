Ideal Teaching

As teaching professionals, we are faced with many challenges when it comes to effective content delivery. Many times, these challenges can seem like burdens that prevent us from being our best versions of ourselves. By taking the time to look at what our ideal teaching situation may be, it can provide us with a lens as to how we can adapt our given situation to best meet the needs of the students.

Ideally, keeping class sizes or sections to a maximum of 40 students can allow enough time for the professor or instructor to be on a first name basis with each of their students. This has the potential benefit of increasing student engagement through comfort with the faculty, and can promote a sense of community within the program. As well with larger class sizes teaching methods as well as instruction approaches change. In research from the IDEA Center and (Inside Higher Ed, 2013) Students in small classes are more likely to be involved in hands on projects and real-life activates.

My program and industry rely heavily on technology. We are an industry of mobile warriors who have the luxury and ability to work remotely if necessary. To that end laptops are a crucial piece of equipment. They not only allow us to be mobile in our careers, they allow us to be mobile with our peers.

Within the Graphic Design program, having a workstation layout would be the most ideal classroom layout. It promotes collaboration, and encourages creativity. An added benefit to this particular layout is the ability to customize the classroom to the needs of the lesson. (Drew, 2021)

According to Stats Canada,  the average age of students are between 17 and 27 years of age (Trends in the Age Composition of College and University Students and Graduates, 2010). When I was a student at 19, I found myself unfocused on my academic career, and more interested in the social aspect of school. It took its toll on my academic career, I returned a little later on, and would say that you should attend school when you are ready to put in the work. (D’Avignon, 2016)

It has been my experience that there isn’t an ideal time to teach. Some students are more effective at night, others during the day. Many have to balance their work schedules with their academic careers. This fact is very apparent  during these unprecedented times. A balanced approach to teaching and learning must be achieved through thoughtful sustainable usage of asynchronous instruction. (Inside Higher Ed, 2020)

Searching for the perfect or ideal teaching situation or environment is like chasing the wind. As educators we must take stock of our available resources adapt where we can, change where we need to, and implement when possible.

I find that my overall communication style is primarily analytical, but personal when necessary. I communicate in very specific language that gets my point across without being mis-interpreted. This allows me to best understand the issue at hand, and makes it easier for me to provide qualitative feedback. However, when approached regarding issues of competency and understanding, I approach these situations with a softer touch.
Communication Styles References

If one of my students is constantly late for class, I provide them with the effects of being late. Specifically, missing out on the lecture intro and activity instructions leads to mis-understanding the activity, which can lead to difficulty later. I would also ask if there is a reason, they are late. There are many factors that may not make themselves present.

When marks are low in the class, I address the entire class and try and drill down to what aspect of the material was the most difficult. During this time assessing what the difficulty was, I do not use phrases such as “The majority of you did poorly”, rather, “This assignment was very challenging” and we review as a class.

If a group approaches me regarding difficulty with a project, I would begin by asking what aspect they are finding challenging. If they are unable to provide a specific reason, I would review the assignment with them to see where the problem arose.

When a student asks me to demonstrate an already understood skill, rather than single out the student, I would demonstrate that skill one-on-one during a class break, or while the remainder of the class is completing an activity.

When it comes to communication inside and outside of the classroom, I make it a point to keep my language positive and avoid any negative words. Whenever possible, I make every effort to sound helpful, and point out the positive aspects. I believe that showing areas of understanding is much stronger then pointing out the obvious deficiencies. This gives the student a sense of empowerment, and a goal to move towards.
