Sample Lesson

Bridge In

For millions of years, humans have been developing their ability to recognize patterns in the natural world. This skill was developed as a way to identify objects in our environment. If an object, plant, or animal was a particular color, size, or smell, we as humans would have the ability to potentially classify it as pleasant, hazardous or potentially life threatening.

Object classification and identification, is not only there to help us identify possible hazards. It is also developed as an ability to recall similar objects, and predict possible outcomes based on given information.

Outcome

The goal of this lesson is to identify what information architecture is, and how it is used in everyday life.

By the end of this activity, participants will be able to:

  • Identify (Ontology, Taxonomy, Choreography)
  • Arrange, and develop methods of information classification
  • Explain the difference between these methods as they apply to organizing information in an online context.

Pre-assessment - Self assessment

  • Are you an organized person
  • Have you ever classified any personal objects
  • What methods did you use to order your belongings? (Ex: Color, size, age, title)

 

Summary

Over the course of many designers' careers, they will be tasked with structuring data in some fashion be that in print or in a digital space. The design of a proper information framework aids the end user by reducing cognitive load, and increasing recognisable patterns. By laying the foundation of how information can be structured, digested, and processed we begin to understand the most effective way to present our content.

Reflection

Over the course of this in class discussion and activity, I found that the students became very engaged with the material. Once they were left to their own devices, they quickly jumped at the chance to discuss their thoughts and possible solutions to the task.

In the past, this particular section was delivered as a self directed exercise. Watching each student actively participate in lively discourse was a welcomed surprise. Seeing the students collectively "get it" was a very simultaneously entertaining, as well as very fulfilling.

I personally believe that engaging students in these types of activities help them gain a deeper understanding of the concept, and goal of the initial lecture.
