Teaching during COVID

Up until last year, when you thought about technology in the classroom, it didn’t go much further then everyone having a computer, and possibly incorporating some form of interactive element to engage students. From Youtube and Microsoft Streams, to OneNote, and Google Drive, as an individual who once thought they were pretty keyed into technology, it became quickly apparent that there are more tools out there that can enhance and enrich the student experience.

Prior to the global pandemic I had initially set out to record and document all of my class material. This decision, and direction was a byproduct of how my classroom was designed. Several rows of tables which can not be moved, do not have a direct line of site to the projected screen. I consider myself lucky that I had this initial deficiency. It afforded me the early opportunity to begin the adoption of digital classroom practices early on.

Currently, during the pandemic it became apparent that although I was using multiple vectors to reach the students, there were more tools available that could again enhance the learning and allow for a richer learning experience.

The main hurdle that needed to be overcome was how to provide feedback to the students in a collaborative and timely manner. My program relies heavily on creative ideation and feedback to support the students with their learning. Thankfully, these tools were almost always available.

By leveraging the use of Microsoft OneNote in conjunction with Microsoft Teams, I was able to have an online class that was almost like being there in real time. The real-time feedback was fantastic. Students were able to get their handouts immediately, as well as collaborate with other students using the digital whiteboard. Breakout rooms became a breeze. No longer did I have to worry about who was picking the group. By allowing teams to randomly select the groups, it significantly reduced the one sided nature of group activities and small discussions. In a digital environment the classroom became a very fair and equitable place.

However with every technological leap forward, there were just as many setbacks. Major technological hurdles had to be overcome. Internet access is not equal. Access to technology and devices is not equal, and not everyone can troubleshoot technical issues and challenges.

These technological issues compounded an issue that affects every classroom regardless of in-person learning; classroom engagement. Many students either had little interest engaging with the class via video chat. As many attended class, but did not engage with class discussions. Lack of microphone or an anxiety of speaking in front of people are often cited as the reason.

During course delivery entertaining students became as important as engaging them with the course material and new tactics needed to be developed. A strategy that proved successful, was an enhanced flipped classroom model. Core theories were broken into digestible segments. These segments took on the feeling of a youtube channel. The idea, meet students where they spend most of their time.

One year into the pandemic, nearly all course delivery methods needed to be adapted. This year has been a challenge to say the least. It is my belief, every challenge has the opportunity to provide a positive outcome. No one can argue that this past year has been difficult, however “under pressure you don’t rise to the occasion, you sink to the level of your training.”
